Background

My career began with a deep interest in the human brain. I've been filled with curiosity around how the human brain works since I was 5, and eventually pursued a Ph.D. in neuroscience to follow that curiosity. Jim Sikela, my Ph.D. mentor, had looked through primate genomes for changes in genomic copy number, and found something very intriguing: a tiny protein domain that increases dramatically in copy number as one gets closer to humans. Not even an entire protein, just a small domain. My dissertation was directed at understanding what these domains are doing. Eventually, we discovered something surprising: the domains are slowing everything down. Humans are known to be very slow to develop as compared to most of the animal kingdom (a phenomenon called "neoteny"). This slowing appears to make it possible to build a massively complex organ like a human brain, in spite of resource and physical constraints.

As a researcher, I developed skills in advanced computational methods, and ultimately put those to work as faculty at The George Washington University. The United States FDA has developed a powerful computational platform called "HIVE" that they use for research and regulatory submissions, but this is an internal system only available to FDA personnel. GW has an agreement to host a publicly available instance of the HIVE platform, and I took over as the lead for this project. As HIVE lead, I expanded the breadth of my research (into the gut brain connection and metagenomics writ large -- fueled by a desire to understand how metabolism is driving brain development and function), my computational skills, and my project management capabilities. Most importantly, I was the face of the project, a role in which I developed my ability to represent highly technical projects at the highest levels to mixed audiences. In this role, I collaborated with FDA dev ops teams, led the development of an international standard, deployed software in highly regulated environments, organized and hosted training workshops, and even prepared public-facing remarks for FDA officials.

The natural extension of my interest in brain science and my computational skills is artificial intelligence. Powered by a deep understanding of the development and function of the human brain and what sets it apart from other animals, I bring a rare perspective to artificial intelligence.

Neuroscience has been the inspiration for artificial intelligence since at least the 1940s, but the comparison is often larger than its given credit for. Attempts to marginalize the similarities between the two often focus on granular, mechanistic details of implementations. But there is great value in considering higher order patters. In understanding that, while implementations differ, goals and constraints are often remarkably similar, and that evolution, with more than 3.5 billion years of problem solving, has martialed available resources against the same constraints.

Big questions are rarely answered all at once. They become answerable by asking better questions. Throughout my career, I've been drawn to problems that are technically complex, scientifically ambiguous, or organizationally difficult to navigate. This website is a collection of those projects, the ideas behind them, and the people and communities that have shaped the journey.

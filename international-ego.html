Can Humanity Agree on What an Intelligent System Should Care About Before We Build Increasingly Capable Ones?

Exploring the challenges of aligning AI systems with human values

A recommendation algorithm optimizes engagement. A company optimizes quarterly earnings. A government optimizes national security. None of those objectives are inherently malicious, but they are different. As increasingly capable AI systems begin acting on behalf of all three, conflicts between objectives become inevitable.

Every sufficiently capable intelligent system optimizes for something. Humans optimize for survival, companies optimize for profit, governments optimize for national interests, and AI systems optimize for the objectives we give them. The question is not whether intelligent systems optimize -- it is whether their objectives remain compatible when they begin interacting.

Historically, disagreements between objectives were mediated by humans. Increasingly capable AI systems may soon execute those objectives at machine speed and global scale, making alignment between institutions more important than ever.

One of evolution's most important discoveries was that cooperation can itself become a successful survival strategy. Groups capable of coordinating their behavior often outperform collections of individually stronger competitors. Human civilization is likely built on that principle.

Standards are one of the few governance mechanisms that have repeatedly succeeded in coordinating independent organizations without requiring centralized control, a form of alliance that individuals, private entities, and governments can all subscribe to. Any actor choosing to ignore those principles would immediately distinguish itself from the broader international consensus.

This idea lives somewhere between the United Nations and technical standards: member entities discuss and develop the technical details according to their priorities, and reach finalization through consensus. Governments, companies, and research organizations could voluntarily adopt such standards in much the same way they adopt technical, safety, and interoperability standards today.

Whether standards ultimately prove to be the right mechanism remains to be seen. Having spent years working on international technical standards and other agreement mechanisms (like public private partnerships), I find standards to be very effective. But whatever form it takes, we will eventually need some shared way of deciding what increasingly capable intelligent systems should value.

An international standard gives us the most flexibility to adapt to different contexts while maintaining a common framework for cooperation. The way in which we prioritize information may be wildly different between individual minds, mid-size companies, and civilizations. Evolution gave individuals a self-model to decide what matters. We now face the analogous challenge of deciding what should matter to the intelligent systems we build together.

The Slow Path to Intelligence

Why would evolution invest so heavily in prolonged development?

Humans spend nearly two decades developing before they begin contributing independently to society. From an evolutionary perspective, that's an astonishing investment. Why would natural selection favor such a slow strategy?

In the field of aging research, one idea that's caught fire is to treat aging like a disease to be cured, bringing with it a framework for how we think about and attempt to cure disease. But another idea that's percolated for a long time is the idea that aging is, itself, selected for by evolution, because turnover is essential for evolution. If correct, this latter idea would mean that aging is an adaptive strategy rather than a pathological condition, something that's built into many, many systems, not a relatively recent byproduct of selecting for something else, which would make it much more difficult to "fight."

Aging is a complex idea that involves many processes. The best we can really do at the moment is to compare milestones between species. When we do that, we find that the pace of a species' aging is correlated with its metabolic rate and lifespan. Some species (like mice) are adapted to very fast-paced lives, and others (like humans) are adapted to very slow-paced lives. Intriguingly, that pace, in development, accounts for an enormous proportion of the physiological differences between species -- and it seems to be controlled by the cell itself.

In recent work (link forthcoming), we tied together three big ideas in this space: why (at the genetic level) humans are so drawn out in their developmental pace compared to other species (called "neoteny"), how this slower pace leads to a bigger brain, and where the primate-enriched, temporary cells that drive all of this ("outer radial glia") arise.

In thinking about biology as a giant optimization problem, as I often do, all of this has gotten me wondering why evolution would select for a species that's so slow that it doesn't even finish development in time (humans are born part way through development), and require nearly two decades of care before they start to develop independently -- longer before they usually join the knowledge economy.

If evolution is indeed selecting for turnover rather than longevity, then a slower developmental pace seems almost paradoxical. Why invest so much time and energy into producing each individual if the system ultimately depends on replacing them?


An obvious analogy for our prolonged development is that our youth is an extended pretraining phase. The enormous investment in resources and time appears to produce a species that exceptionally capable at flexible thinking and abstraction, and which has a larger repertoire of ideas to recombine to produce novel concepts.

It's a loose analogy for several anatomical reasons, but potentially a useful one: if life really is selecting for turnover, then habitual pretraining may actually be part of the selection pressure on drawing out development for so long, not the "construction of large brains" so commonly attributed to it. If recent discussions around AI scaling laws are substantially correct -- that capability grows only logarithmically with compute -- then evolution's apparent preference for many independent, repeatedly trained agents becomes even more intriguing. Lots of smaller, independent agents, and turnover (periodically retraining them) keeps flexibility and the ability to adapt to a dynamic world high.

Every individual in a species has to become capable of navigating their environment, and species that occupy broad, diverse environments, need more flexible thinking. The more flexible our thinking, the more diverse the environments we can occupy. Our curiosity might be part of a beautiful positive feedback loop that extends our development, our lives, and our capacity for curiosity.

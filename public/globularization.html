Globularization Makes Humans an Ensemble Method

I've written about how primate brains are unique in the animal kingdom (and what we can infer from them about the design of transformers). From this same argument, it has been argued that human brains are not unique.

From a purely neuron number perspective, this is true: the human brain is just a linearly scaled up primate brain. However, perhaps unsurprisingly, we are not identical to our primate relatives in terms of cognitive abilities (as far as we know). There does seem to be something unique about us.

In fact, there may be multiple things about us that are unique, this is very much an active area of research (and one of my favorites). But there is one way in which the human brain does seem to be different that we do have some evidence for: the overall shape.

Before I go any further, I want to immediately acknowledge and distance myself from any discussion of phrenology, the long discredited idea that inter-indivual differences in skull shape correspond to mental faculties, long a tool of racism.

Instead what I'm referring to is "globularization," the more spherical brain shape that we (ALL humans) have.

One influential hypothesis is that the more spherical human brain facilitates more efficient communication between distant cortical regions, potentially supporting the kinds of large-scale integration required for language, abstract reasoning, and social cognition. The logic for this is that the average path length between cortical regions is reduced with a spherical shape, making inter-areal connections more efficient. This is critical for building a world view: all regions of the brain need to be able to communicate effectively so that hierarchichal ideas can be built effectively.

The "connections" I'm referring to are the neural pathways that allow different parts of the brain to communicate. Schüz and Braitenberg discovered in 2002 that humans have a natural inverse proportion in these pathways: the longer the fiber, the fewer of them there are. This may be a natural optimization for energy efficiency, as long fibers are metabolically expensive (therefore biasing for local processing).

Evidence that the ratio of these connections is important for social communication might be seen by the fact that individuals with autism and schizophrenia (disorders in which social cognition is frequently affected) are associated with alterations in this architecture. In autism, a local over-proliferation of neurons or neural connections seems to interfere with the long range connections (i.e. the proportion shifts towards short range fibers). In this proposed framework, the result is an architecture that hyper processes signals at the expense of a larger integration. In schizophrenia, the proportion may be shifted the other way: fewer short range connections (resulting in an enlarged ventricle, one of the most replicated features of schizophrenia). In this architecture, signals are integrated before they're even finished processing locally, making even basic aspects difficult to determine (such as whether the signal originated internally or from the outside world).

Bonobos, the poster child for pro-social primates, are so genetically similar to chimpanzees that the idea of calling them separate species has repeatedly been challenged. Bonobos have also been noted to possess a more globular cranial morphology than chimpanzees.

Among the most interesting of the arguments that globularization is important for social cognition are the archeological and physiological comparisons with Neanderthals. Neanderthals had brains as large as humans (and often larger), and there are several signs that they were very intelligent.

What may have distinguished modern humans was the scale over which cooperation could be maintained. Compared with early modern humans, Neanderthal populations appear to have maintained smaller, less interconnected, and less persistent social networks (one researcher said that they would seem to us like xenophobic geniuses). As it turns out, Neanderthal skulls were also far more dolichocephaclic (elongated like an American football).

It's impossible to know anything about Neanderthal social habits with certainty or why they went extinct since we have to rely on the archeological record, but at this moment in time, evidence points to social communication and cooperation as a key factor in explaining why a large-brained primate with lots of computational units thrived.

One of the most interesting implications for AI is that evolution appears to have discovered the value of distributing cognition across many partially independent agents with different local information and different heuristics, while maintaining efficient communication between them. Modern ensemble methods succeed for mathematically similar reasons: independently imperfect models make different errors. Human societies may operate similarly. Each individual possesses incomplete information, different heuristics, and different experiences. By communicating, disagreeing, and pooling those perspectives, groups often arrive at better solutions than any individual could alone. If evolution repeatedly converged on distributed cognition as a solution to complex reasoning, it raises an intriguing possibility: the future of AI may depend not only on building better individual models, but on discovering better ways for many specialized models to reason together.

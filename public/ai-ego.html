Our Egos Were Built for Survival, But They Might Get In The Way of It

As I sit at my desk and look at my computer monitor, I can see a plant, a table full of my son's toys, a vintage poster advertising Ski Colorado, and a piano (not a humble brag, I'm pretty mediocre). I can also hear the background noise of cars and construction in the distance outside, if I stop and pay attention. In fact, I can see the fibers in the carpet near me, the wrinkles in a throw pillow, and broad swaths of light and dark patterns on the wall. My brain is receiving dozens of signals that it's essentially not paying any attention to.

It turns out that human brains are receiving about 11 million inputs in any given instant (not per second, but live streaming that many channels). Estimates vary depending on how we define an independent "signal," but the point is that the volume of data that we process is bananas. Far, far more than a conscious mind could ever attend to.

So how do we do it? Well, clearly I'm not paying any attention to the vast majority of it, so I've likely got some way of triaging all of this information and raking "what matters," as determined by my own internal models. The most natural way to do this is to relate it to the self.

The self is the one thing that will always be with us, for as long as we can think. So this gives us a natural, built in way of ranking the importance of all of that information. How does it relate to me? First and foremost, is it an immediate threat to my own survival? Second, is it a long term threat? Third, is it an opportunity?

That may not be the exact order of operations (or even the exact steps), but I imagine that some sort of triaging process like this is very likely. So now I've got an elegant way of ignoring 42,000 Legos and focusing on my writing.

A prioritization system needs a reference frame. "Important" only has meaning relative to something. In biological organisms, that reference frame is naturally the organism itself. In a computational theory of consciousness, it's hard not to come to the conclusion that this ordering of data is the ego. The self is the most important thing in the universe, because the entire model only exists if the self exists. My conception of the universe is part of my "self." Without all of that ranking, events simply happen.

Businesses and other institutions have the ability to write these triage rules explicitly, and to therefore create a more thoughtful rule book. But institutional priorities will always be their own survival and success (ironically built from human egos). So if we're not careful, "thoughtful" rulemaking may give way to the ever persistent optimization of evolution: survival of the fittest.

This may be one of the most fundamental challenges of our time. How do we establish a set of rules for information prioritization that doesn't build synthetic egos that compete for dominance? How do we enforce that solution? Is it even realistic to think that we can?

Every intelligent system needs some way of deciding what matters. For biological organisms, evolution provided a remarkably effective solution: put the self at the center. As we begin designing increasingly capable artificial intelligences, we're faced with an unusual challenge. We no longer have to inherit evolution's priorities, we get to choose them.

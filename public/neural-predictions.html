The Vast Majority of Our Synapses Are Probably Prediction Machines

Commentaries on next token prediction -- the fundamental task of LLMs -- range from derision ("it's just predicting the next token") to claims that it is understanding.

Human brains are estimated to have anywhere from 100 trillion to nearly 1 quadrillion synapses, depending on who you ask. To put that number in context, if you examined one synapse every second, around the clock without ever stopping, you would still be counting 16 million years from now.

"Neat," would be a reasonable response from an outsider. It's a science factoid that involves a big number, and those are plentiful these days. A neater fact is that only a minority (thought to be only around 10%) are positioned close enough to the soma (the neural cell body) to exert strong direct influence over action potential (the electrical impulse that acts like a binary signal) initiation.

For years there was debate over what these vast numbers of distal synapses were actually doing. Some scientists even dismissed them as noise, a necessary consequence of the brain feeling its way through the world through trial and error. But the brain is plastic; it can build and break down synapses as needed. There's no need to keep synapses that aren't used. Further, the human brain eats somewhere in the range of 20-25% of our daily caloric intake, which is an enormous number, and a huge part of that budget goes towards maintaining synapses. Why on Earth would we invest so heavily in building and maintaining that many synapses if we're not using them for something important?

One response to this that's gained a lot of attention lately is Jeff Hawkins' idea that they're prediction machines (I mostly recommend the first half of that book). Essentially, he argues that all of these distant synapses are priming the neuron to be ready to fire. It's like a runner getting into the ready position: when you crouch down and hear "ready, set...", you've got a huge advantage over other runners who are caught completely off guard by a starting pistol -- so much so that they might not even run the race.

Prediction allows computation to begin before the world reveals the answer. Instead of merely reacting, the brain is constantly preparing candidate interpretations and actions, dramatically reducing the time required to respond when one of them proves correct. The idea is that they're sensitive to some pattern that predicts some outcome, but not 100% of the time. So when they see that pattern, they (and others in the pipeline) get the neuron ready, in case it's needed. We don't realize it's happening until we go to pick up a coffee cup to take a drink and discover that it's empty and we pick it up much faster than we had unconsciously planned to do. Motor commands, opposing muscles, and postural adjustments were all calibrated for an expected weight, and other, competing circuits, weren't. The human brain is constantly observing patterns in the world and making predictions so that the human can efficiently navigate it.

To say that next token prediction is understanding implies that we know more about consciousness than we do. That's a rabbit hole of speculation that this post isn't prepared to enter. Intelligence (the human kind, not the log of compute kind) are to neuroscience as "dark matter" is to physics: we think there's something there, so we give it a name that lets us talk about it, and we have plenty of ideas as to what it could be, but we really have no idea what it is. Our definitions will almost certainly change over time as we learn more about it. Chances are that there are more than one kind of "understanding," and that it's not binary, so even saying that it is or isn't understanding is, itself, too reductive.

But it isn't too reductive to say that next token prediction is a fundamental task of human brains. If Hawkins is substantially correct, then a large fraction of our synapses may exist primarily to implement prediction. So if our brains are built on a platform that's 90% comprised of next token prediction, next-token prediction may be far more central to biological intelligence than it's often given credit for. If that's true, modern LLMs may already embody one of the brain's fundamental computational principles. The remaining challenge is understanding everything else that biological intelligence adds on top. Now we just have to figure out the remaining 10%.

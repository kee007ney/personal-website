My Story

Background

My career began with a deep interest in the human brain. I've been filled with curiosity around how the human brain works and how intelligence emerges since I was 5, and eventually pursued a Ph.D. in neuroscience to follow that curiosity. Jim Sikela, my Ph.D. mentor, had looked through primate genomes for changes in genomic copy number, and found something very intriguing: a tiny protein domain that increases dramatically in copy number as one gets closer to humans. Not even an entire protein, just a small domain. My dissertation was directed at understanding what these domains are doing. Eventually, we discovered something surprising: the domains are slowing everything down. Humans are known to be very slow to develop as compared to most of the animal kingdom (a phenomenon called "neoteny"). This slowing appears to make it possible to build a massively complex organ like a human brain, in spite of resource and physical constraints.

As a graduate student researcher, I developed skills in advanced computational methods, and ultimately put those to work as faculty at The George Washington University. The United States FDA has developed a powerful computational platform called "HIVE" that they use for research and regulatory submissions, but this is an internal system only available to FDA personnel. GW has an agreement to host a publicly available instance of the HIVE platform, and I took over as the lead for this project. As HIVE lead, I expanded my computational skills, project management capabilities, and the breadth of my research (into the gut brain connection and metagenomics writ large -- fueled by a desire to understand how metabolism is driving brain development and function). In many settings, I became the public facing lead for the project, a role in which I developed my ability to represent technical projects at the highest levels to mixed audiences. In this role, I collaborated with FDA dev ops teams, led the development of an international standard, deployed software in highly regulated environments, built user stories, organized and hosted training workshops, gathered usage data, and even prepared public-facing remarks for FDA officials.

The natural extension of my interest in brain science and computational biology is artificial intelligence. I do not see AI as a departure from neuroscience, but as another way of asking questions about intelligence, learning, representation, and constraint.

Neuroscience has inspired artificial intelligence since at least the 1940s, but the comparison is often treated too narrowly. The implementations differ, of course, but the higher-order problems can be remarkably similar. Both biological and artificial systems must learn from limited information, allocate resources, generalize from experience, and operate under constraints. Evolution has spent more than 3.5 billion years exploring that design space.

Big questions are rarely answered all at once. They become answerable by asking better questions. Throughout my career, I've been drawn to problems that are technically complex, scientifically ambiguous, or organizationally difficult to navigate. This website is a collection of those projects, the ideas behind them, and the people and communities that have shaped the journey.

Methods

I enjoy starting with questions that feel too broad, too ambiguous, or too technically challenging to answer directly. Rather than trying to solve them all at once, I try to impose structure: identify the underlying questions, develop practical ways to investigate them, and build solutions that help others explore them as well.

Sometimes those solutions are software platforms. Sometimes they're data models, analytical methods, technical standards, or educational programs. Increasingly, they're AI-enabled workflows. The medium changes; the underlying process does not.

That philosophy has shaped every major project I've worked on, from studying the evolution of the human brain to developing regulatory software platforms, leading international technical standards, and helping scientific communities adopt emerging technologies. The questions and the tools will continue to evolve, but the curiosity remains the same.
